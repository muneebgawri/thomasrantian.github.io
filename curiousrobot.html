Curious minded intelligent robots in interactive and uncertain environments

Introduction

Curiosity is closely linked to the learning processes in humans and other animals, and it has been hypothesized that curiosity is a key component of general intelligence. Here we take the view that curious behavior optimizes an intrinsic goal to actively collect information from the environment. This strategy has recently shown great improvements in Reinforcement Learning (RL). In this work, we show that these benefits extend to other forms of motion planning, where curiosity gives us a better means to reason about uncertainty by actively seeking to improve our understanding.


Approach

To realize our curious agent, we combine partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) with receding horizon optimization, and information theory to enable a continually evolving online decision strategy. A common approach to handle planning in the face of uncertainty is to use partially observable Monte-Carlo planning (POMCP). POMCP uses Monte-Carlo samples to simultaneously improve beliefs and search for optimal behaviors, and is known to converge to an optimal policy when the belief is correct. However errors in the belief degrade performance. When coupled with the computation limits of running online, other strategies become preferable. Open-loop planning algorithms that seek optimal sequences of actions, have been shown to perform well and sometimes optimally in these situations. And, like our work, have been applied to autonomous driving domains. Unlike Yu et al., our approach allows for stochastic actions.
